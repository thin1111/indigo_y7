The Internet is the global system of interconnected computer networks. It is a network of networks that consists of private, public, academic, business, and government networks of local to global scope, linked by a broad array of electronic and wireless, networking technologies. The Internet carries a vast range of information resources and services. The Internet has transformed the world of computers and communication in ways never seen before. Earlier innovations like the telegraph, telephone, radio, and computer laid the foundation for this remarkable fusion of technologies. Today, the Internet acts as a worldwide hub for communication, information exchange, and seamless collaboration between individuals and their devices, no matter where they are. It exemplifies the incredible outcomes of continuous investment and commitment to advancing technological infrastructure. In 1973, the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) launched a research initiative to explore methods for connecting different types of packet networks. The goal was to create communication protocols that would enable seamless interaction between computers across interconnected networkThis initiative, known as the Internetting project, ultimately gave rise to what we now call the "Internet." It also led to the creation of the TCP/IP Protocol Suite, which is named after its two core protocols: Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) and Internet Protocol (IP).

Malware, short for malicious software, is a kind of harmful code or program that can damage. Researchers often categorise malware into different types, such as computer viruses, worms, Trojan horses, ransomware, spyware, adware and keyloggers. Malware can access, scramble, or erase information, and lock a device. It can also show unwanted ads, make the device easier to hack, or change how it works. Malware can spread when you open an infected file, run a program, or start a script. Malware refers to harmful software created to compromise or damage computer systems and networks.
